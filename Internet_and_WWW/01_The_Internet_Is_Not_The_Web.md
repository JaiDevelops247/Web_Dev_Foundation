## Introduction

Here is a question: When you open Chrome and visit YouTube, are you using the Internet or the World Wide Web?

Most people say **The Internet.** And most people are wrong.

This is one of the most common misconceptions in tech, and it is completely understandable because we use both terms every day as if they mean the same thing. They do not. The Internet and the World Wide Web are two different things, built at different times, by different people, for different reasons.

By the end of this reading, you will be able to tell anyone exactly what the difference is, and you will never mix them up again.

### Why Did We Even Need Any of This?

To understand the difference, you need to go back to a time when neither existed.

In the 1950s and 1960s, Computers were large, expensive, and completely isolated. Each machine was its own island. If a researcher at one university wanted to share data with a researcher at another, they physically mailed a magnetic tape. No exaggeration.

The problem was obvious: **Computers needed a way to talk to each other.**

Meanwhile, governments, universities, and militaries had a second, more urgent problem. During the Cold War, the US military realised that their communication systems were fragile. A single point of failure could cut off critical information. They needed a network that could survive damage and reroute itself automatically.

These two problems, sharing data and building a resilient communication system, led to the creation of the Internet.



### How the Internet Was Born

In 1969, a project called **ARPANET** (Advanced Research Projects Agency Network), funded by the US Department of Defense, successfully sent the first message between two computers located at different universities.

It crashed after two letters. The word was "LOGIN" and only "LO" got through before the system failed. But it worked in principle, and that was enough.

Over the next two decades, more universities and research institutions connected to this growing network. Rules were created for how data should be broken into packets, sent across the network, and reassembled at the destination. 

These rules are called **protocols**, and the most important ones are called **TCP/IP** (Transmission Control Protocol / Internet Protocol).

By the late 1980s, a global network of networks had formed. Cables under oceans, satellites in orbit, routers in data centers, all working together. **This infrastructure is the Internet.**

But at this point, it was mostly just scientists and engineers using it to send emails and transfer files. It had no pages to browse, no links to click, no websites to visit.

### Then Came the Web

In 1989, a British scientist named **Tim Berners-Lee** was working at CERN, the physics research lab in Switzerland. Scientists there had a different problem: They were generating massive amounts of research, and there was no easy way to link related documents together or share them across institutions.

His proposal was simple but revolutionary: Create a system of documents that could link to each other using something called **hyperlinks**, all accessible over the Internet via browser.

He called it the **World Wide Web**.

In **1991**, the first website went live. It was a plain text page explaining what the World Wide Web project was. Nothing flashy. But it changed everything.

The key insight: **The Web was built on top of the Internet.** It did not replace it. It used the existing Internet infrastructure as its road, and built a new kind of destination on it.

This is the easiest way to lock in the difference.

Imagine a massive city with a highway system connecting every neighbourhood, every town, every country in the world. The highways are always running. They carry cars, trucks, and buses. You use them to get from place to place.

Now imagine all the shops, restaurants, schools, cinemas, and libraries built alongside those highways. These are the places you actually visit and use.

**The Internet is the highway system.**
Cables under oceans, routers in data centers, satellites in orbit. The physical and logical infrastructure that moves data from one device to another.

**The World Wide Web is the shops along the highway.**
Websites, web pages, and web applications that you visit using a browser.

You need the highways to reach the shops. But the highway and the shops are completely different things. And here is the part most people miss: **not everything on the highway is a shop.** Trucks carry goods. Buses carry people. Ambulances rush to emergencies. They all use the highway, but they are not shops.

Similarly:
- An email travels over the Internet but is not part of the web.
- A WhatsApp message travels over the Internet but is not part of the web.
- A Zoom call travels over the Internet but is not part of the web.
- An FTP file transfer travels over the Internet but is not part of the web.

All of these use the internet. None of them are the web.

### Let's Define Things Now

Now that the analogy is in place, here are the definitions to remember:

> **The Internet:** A global system of interconnected computer networks that use standardized communication protocols (primarily TCP/IP) to link devices worldwide.

In plain terms: The physical and logical infrastructure connecting billions of devices. Cables, routers, satellites, and the rules that let data move between them.

> **The World Wide Web (WWW):** A system of interlinked hypertext documents and multimedia content, accessed via the internet using a web browser.

In plain terms: Every website and web page you visit through a browser like Chrome, Safari, or Firefox.



### What Each One Actually Does

| | Internet | World Wide Web |
|---|---|---|
| What is it? | Global network infrastructure | System of linked web pages and sites |
| How do you access it? | Any device that connects online | A web browser |
| Who created it? | ARPANET and evolved over decades | Tim Berners-Lee, 1989 |
| Examples of use | Email, WhatsApp, video calls, gaming, FTP | Any website: Google, YouTube, Wikipedia |
| Requires a browser? | No | Yes |
| Can exist without the other? | Yes, Internet existed before the web | No, the Web needs the Internet to function |

The most important row is the last one. The Internet can and did exist without the Web. But the Web cannot exist without the Internet.

### Activity: Quick-Fire Tag

Test yourself. For each scenario below, label it as **"Internet"** or **"WWW"** and write one sentence explaining why.

1. You open Chrome and visit amazon.com to buy something.
2. You send a voice note to a friend on WhatsApp.
3. You join a Zoom call with your team.
4. You Google the weather for tomorrow.
5. You upload project files to an FTP server.

Think through each one before checking. The key question to ask yourself is: **Am I using a browser to visit a web page?** If yes, it is the WWW. If not, it is the Internet but not the web.
